# When the Data Lies

This is a case study in measurement discipline on a real-money system that hasn't yet spent real money. The system underneath it is a cross-venue arbitrage engine: it watches two prediction-market exchanges – Kalshi and Polymarket US – for the same real-world event priced differently, and when buying both sides would lock a profit no matter how the event resolves, it's built to fire both legs in under a tenth of a second.

Finance interested me because it's probably the most adversarial arena there is to build software for: the counterparty is the entire market, every mistake is priced in real dollars, and the field is crowded with extremely well-capitalized institutions with far more resources than just a laptop. Building something from scratch, on my own, that actually holds up in that environment is exactly the kind of hard, real-world problem I like solving. The goal of this engine is to make money, but not only for the sake of making money. "Did it make money" is the most honest test of whether or not I've fulfilled my goal.

## The thesis: distrust your own detector

A naïve version of this project detects edges, fires trades, and checks the bank balance. That design folds three independent failure modes – the edge wasn't real, the edge was real but couldn't be filled, the fill was right but settlement diverged – into a single number, and only tells you anything *after* real money was already spent to learn which one killed you.

So, I inverted it. Instead of deploying live capital from day one, the engine runs in a dry-run mode that simulates the full detection and fire-path logic against live market data and logs what *would* have happened. This mode uses the same fresh reads a real order would race against, the same gates, but with no order placed. The only live orders sent have been a handful of tiny, deliberate probes to ground-truth venue behavior (exact fees, order latency, how the venue actually handles an order type) – these are described in depth below. Everything else is measurement: a growing dataset about whether the edges are real, fillable, and durable through settlement. The whole apparatus is built to answer one question honestly before I spend anything: When my detector says it found a profit, is it telling the truth?

## The system lied to me, and I caught it

There have been four times the detector reported a profit that wasn't there. Each one has the same shape: something looked real, a measurement contradicted it, I found the mechanism, fixed it, and verified the fix rather than assuming it.

**The stale-price mirage.** Polymarket serves its order books through a CDN that caches responses for thirty seconds. My fire path was reading what looked like live prices, when they could be half a minute stale. A stale price on one venue against a fresh price on the other looks exactly like a large potential edge and is entirely fake – in one logged case the cached book reported 726,257 contracts of depth where the real book held 3. The fix was a one-line cache-bust (each read gets a unique throwaway value added to its URL, so the CDN can't serve a stale copy and has to fetch fresh), but the important part was the verification. Rather than trust a plausible fix, I confirmed it changed what came back. Reading the same market repeatedly through the old path returned identical snapshots the CDN flagged as cache hits; the new read came back a cache miss – served fresh – and tracked the live book, while the cached copy had drifted from it by over a cent. The fix was real because I watched the behavior change, not because the diff looked right. 

**The matcher hedging the wrong game.** To hedge, you must pair the *same* real-world event across two venues – and the ways that pairing can go wrong are subtle. First it went wrong by name: when I added the WNBA to discovery, a 33.8% "edge" appeared. "Minnesota" matches to a baseball team and a basketball team, and the matcher had paired a Polymarket MLB market against a Kalshi WNBA market – two different games, their unrelated prices subtracted. Later it went wrong by time: for a doubleheader (two games between the same teams on one day), the matcher paired one venue's game 1 against the other's game 2. That one was not cosmetic – **149 of 264** logged "guaranteed profit" opportunities were this bug: not arbitrage at all, but a bet between two unrelated games. The fix was a check on start times – markets can't be the same game if their start times can't reconcile – and it's fail-closed: a sport or pairing the matcher can't positively verify matches *nothing*, rather than risk another ghost.

**The API docs were wrong, and only a real order proved it.** Polymarket's published reference documents fill-or-kill orders – "must fill entirely or cancel." The exchange doesn't implement it. It silently rewrites a fill-or-kill order to immediate-or-cancel (fill as much as possible) and fills it partially, with no error and no warning. This is more than just a technicality: fill-or-kill is what lets you treat a leg as all-or-nothing, and if a leg you believe is all-or-nothing can actually fill halfway, the hedge math is broken and
you can be stuck carrying a naked, one-sided position. I found it with a small real order, a fill-or-kill order for 300 shares into a
market holding only 255. It should have come back as zero, it filled 255. And the raw response was deceptive even then: its first execution record reported a fill of 0, with the real 255 buried in a later record, so a parser that trusted the obvious field would have concluded "the leg missed" on a leg that filled. I built the execution path around the behavior the API *has*, confirmed against captured real responses, not the behavior it's *documented* to have.

**The guard that silently did nothing.** The engine has a reconciler that asks each venue what positions I actually hold and compares it against what the engine believes – protection against a silently stranded position. Its Kalshi half fell into a trap I now watch for everywhere: it pulled the positions out of the response with a defaulted lookup, so when the response shape wasn't what the code assumed, the default turned "I don't understand this" into "you hold nothing" – and the safety net reported "flat" on every poll, no matter what the actual positions were. It stayed invisible because every test mocked the reconciler's own read, so the fixtures encoded the same wrong assumption the code did and agreed with the bug by construction – the exact failure this project is otherwise built to prevent. The fix is less about the field than the discipline: it now raises on any response shape it doesn't recognize, so an unparseable response pauses trading as "I cannot verify," never a convenient "flat."

## Chasing the phantom

The bug episodes are about distrusting the system. This one is about the harder discipline: distrusting my own conclusions, in public, more than once.

Early on, the dry-run data showed a low fill-success rate – most detected edges, re-checked against the real order book at the instant an order would land, had no liquidity behind them. The obvious first hypothesis was thin books – there weren't any shares for me to buy. I wrote a probe to test it: compare each venue quote against the real, freshly-read order book. Result – **0 of 120** sampled quotes were unbacked; the books were deep, hundreds of contracts a side. Thin books, refuted.

Once I found this, I got too confident. I wrote up the result as "the edges are capturable, the bug is a fixable price feed" and committed it. A few hours later, I caught a measurement error I'd missed earlier – my probe had compared the REST *quote* against the REST *order book*, both from the same source, when the phantom actually came from a *different* feed (the streaming ticker channel the detector actually fires on). The probe had confirmed something true and irrelevant. The claim was downgraded to an inference and a second probe was made mandatory before any fix. That second probe read the actual ticker channel: **0 of 34** unbacked on random markets. A generally-stale feed, also refuted.

So: random markets, both probes, quotes backed and books deep. But the edges my detector *selected* – **17 of 17** in the first sample, and across a later, larger, deduplicated dataset roughly **99%** of the dominant failure mode – were unbacked. The two facts only reconcile one way, and it's the insight the whole project turns on: **the detector selects the moments the feed is momentarily wrong.** A lagging quote is *exactly* what manufactures an apparent arbitrage, so the scanner fires precisely on those instants. Random markets look clean because they aren't detection-selected. It's a selection effect – the same reason a strategy that looks strong in a backtest of its own winners could actually be weak. Deduplicated to distinct economic states, roughly **69%** of everything the detector flags is this illusion. That reframed the roadmap: the lever isn't faster execution or a fixable feed, it's
pricing detection off the real order book so the phantoms are never generated.

A sub-plot from the same stretch is the cleanest single example of believing the instrument over the hope. I'd suspected a specific leak: a frozen feed re-sending a stale price with a *fresh* timestamp on every message, which would sail through a staleness gate that only checks timestamp recency. It was a plausible failure and I was about to build a gate against it. But I took the time to build a probe to test the premise first – and it refuted the premise. The venue's book timestamp turned out to be a *last-mutation* stamp, not a re-send clock: across dozens of byte-identical books it never advanced once. So it *is* a genuine content-freshness signal, and the gate I was about to ship would have guarded a failure mode that doesn't happen in steady state. I didn't build it. What I built instead is a narrower guard that only rejects a book when its timestamp is genuinely old *and* several other markets are simultaneously frozen – the unambiguous signature of a feed-wide outage – and it has since fired correctly in production. 

## Where I corrected myself on the numbers

**The latency reversal.** I nearly abandoned the strategy on a latency assumption. Detected edges in my sample live only about 0.3 seconds, and I'd assumed the round trip to verify and fire both legs was in the same range – so I expected edges to evaporate before I could act. Measurement corrected me. The verify round-trip is about 30 ms in-region; the two order legs, measured on production with zero-fill probe orders that place nothing real, land in about 78 ms total – both comfortably inside the ~0.3 s the
edges actually persist (they held ≥ 280 ms in the sample). Colocating the bot in-region rather than running from my laptop cut the slow leg's round-trip several-fold. The constraint I'd talked myself out of the strategy over wasn't the binding one – which is genuinely useful, because it moved the real question to where it belongs: not "can I act in time" but "when my order lands, is the liquidity still there on both legs at size." That's fillability, and it's the open question the dry-run measurement exists to answer.

There's a recurring pattern across all of this worth naming, because it's the actual skill on display: every time a hopeful claim showed up – "capturable," "fixable feed," "frozen serve-time feed" – it got caught and downgraded to an inference until a measurement settled it. Twice that catch came from review; once I caught myself against the raw data; once a probe refuted a premise I was about to build on. The same discipline extends to the documentation: the project's own notes carry provenance tags marking each claim as verified-against-code, asserted-but-unconfirmed, or confirmed-against-venue-docs, and keeping
those honest has repeatedly surfaced latent bugs that stale constants were papering over. Writing down how confident you're *allowed* to be about each fact is what separates a system you trust from one you hope about.

## The engineering that makes the measurements trustworthy

The methodology rests on infrastructure that's deliberately careful.

The feeds distinguish three different ways a connection can fail, because collapsing them hides the dangerous one. A dropped socket is caught by protocol keepalives. A connected-but-silent stream is a separate timer. And a *frozen* book – messages still flowing, prices never changing – is a third detector watching for real content changes, because that's the failure that silently feeds phantom edges into detection while looking perfectly healthy. Most systems check only "is it connected" and miss the zombie.

The profit accounting keeps three buckets that are never netted against each other: realized execution cost, the *booked* edge at fill (which is not yet money – settlement can still erode it), and the true post-settlement result. Only the last one is allowed to size the next trade. It's easy to fool yourself by treating a booked edge as profit; the discipline is refusing to let anything but settled reality influence how much capital goes in next.

Two habits keep the tests honest. The money path is *mutation-tested*: for each safety-critical fix, reverting it must fail a specific test – a suite that still passes with the fix backed out is treated as no coverage. And venue behavior is pinned against *captured real responses*, not hand-written fixtures, because a fixture written from the same belief as the code can only ever confirm it. The reconciler bug above is exactly what that guards against: its tests were green for weeks against a fixture that encoded the wrong response shape.

And the risk that would actually cost money if it were wrong – that two markets I hedge might settle to different outcomes – is measured, not assumed. Two markets are paired only after a rules-text check that they resolve on the same official basis, and anything unverified is blocked rather than traded. Across **408 settled games** (MLB, WNBA, World Cup; about four weeks) the paired markets have **never once settled to conflicting payouts** – zero divergences, zero voids – including World Cup group games that can end in a draw. That's the one number here that shows a core assumption *holding* rather than a bug getting caught, and it's the evidence that the settlement leg of the risk is real-but-bounded rather than open-ended.

## The verdict is pending – and that's a real answer

The question the apparatus exists to answer is narrow: **are the detected edges fillable, at a size worth deploying, at real latency – and do they survive settlement?** Latency is cleared. Settlement equivalence holds so far. The open one is fillability – whether the roughly a third of detected edges that aren't stale-feed illusions can actually be taken at deployable size when an order lands – and that data is still accumulating. The fix that should move it (pricing detection off the order book, so the phantoms never enter the sample) is measured and ready; the go-live decision is gated on the evidence, not a date.

I've built the rest of the system to be indifferent to which way that lands. If the edges prove capturable and settle clean, capital goes in with the risks already characterized. If they don't, that's a strategy retired on evidence before a dollar was exposed to it – which is a complete result, and a cheaper one than the alternative. The goal is profit; the discipline is that I'll only chase it once the instrument I built to distrust says the profit is real. Being willing to hear it say no is part of the same discipline, not a hedge against it.
